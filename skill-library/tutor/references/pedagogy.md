# Pedagogical Background

## Relational vs. instrumental understanding (Skemp, 1976)

Richard Skemp drew a sharp line between two kinds of understanding:

**Instrumental** — knowing a rule that produces an answer ("do step X") without knowing why. Feels efficient; is brittle. Forgotten after the moment, builds no foundation for unfamiliar problems. Most instruction produces this, because it's faster to teach and test.

**Relational** — "knowing what to do *and* why" — a connected mental model the learner can generate their own solutions from, for problems they've never seen. Takes longer to build; transfers broadly.

Self-learners with no instructor are especially at risk: they may not know which kind of understanding they have until a novel problem exposes the gap.

**Implication:** Never teach a step as a bare instruction. Teach the reasoning that produces the step, and name the thinking move so it transfers.

## The illusion of competence

Learners systematically overestimate what they understand. A clear explanation *feels* like learning, so "I followed that" gets mistaken for "I can do that." Recognition (following along) and recall (reproducing from memory) feel identical in the moment; they are not. Only recall and transfer build durable ability.

Research on AI-assisted learning makes this worse: fluent, frictionless output is easy to accept passively. Learners who lean on AI for answers show measurable declines in their own ability.

The fix is not to be a worse explainer. It is to **make the learner retrieve and reconstruct**, not just receive.

**Implication:** End explanations with reconstruction tasks and twist problems, not "does that make sense?" The goal is a learner who needs you slightly less each session.

## Cognitive load theory (Sweller)

Working memory holds roughly 4 chunks simultaneously. When a learner is overwhelmed, learning stops entirely. Signs of overload: blank responses, "I don't know where to start," flailing without direction.

The fix is not to explain more or louder — it is to **narrow scope**: one sub-problem, one concept, one decision per turn.

**Implication:** Don't present the full solution path. Don't list five considerations at once. Ask one question per turn. If the learner is overloaded, find the single next step they can actually take.

## Zone of Proximal Development (Vygotsky)

The ZPD is the gap between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with support. Learning happens in this zone — not below it (too easy, no growth) and not above it (too far ahead, pure confusion).

The tutor's job is to locate the ZPD and work there. This is not static: it shifts as the learner grows, and it shifts *within a single session*.

**Implication:** Calibrate constantly. Hints not landing after two turns → the break is lower than you thought; drop a rung. Learner breezing through → too far below ZPD; raise difficulty.

## Productive struggle vs. unproductive frustration

Struggle is good — it signals to the brain that something is worth encoding. But there is a threshold past which struggle becomes demoralization: the learner stops trying not because the problem is hard, but because they feel incapable.

Calibrating this threshold is the central skill of a tutor:
- Hints not landing after two turns → the break is lower than you thought; drop a rung and rediagnose.
- Rising emotional language ("I'm stupid," "I'll never get this") → redirect to the concrete immediately; one achievable sub-task.
- Repeated identical error after two corrections → it's a misconception, not a slip; see `obstacles.md`.

## Why "does that make sense?" fails

It is a yes/no question with strong social pressure toward yes. The learner who is lost says yes because saying no feels like admitting failure. The learner who understood also says yes. The tutor receives zero information either way.

Replace it with reconstruction: "Without scrolling up, what were the two key moves?" This question cannot be answered with a social reflex — it requires retrieval. A blank or uncertain answer is the signal you needed.

## Bloom's Taxonomy — levels of understanding

Understanding has depth. From shallow to deep:

1. **Remember** — can recite the rule or fact
2. **Understand** — can explain it in their own words
3. **Apply** — can use it in a familiar context
4. **Analyze** — can break it down, compare it to alternatives
5. **Evaluate** — can judge when to use it and when not to
6. **Create** — can produce something new using the principle

Most learners stop at level 2 and mistake it for level 4. The tutor's job is to push past recognition toward application and analysis.

**Implication:** "Does that make sense?" probes level 1–2. A twist problem probes level 3–4. "When would this fail?" probes level 5. Target the level above where the learner currently sits.
