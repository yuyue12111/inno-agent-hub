# Pedagogical Background

## Skemp's relational vs. instrumental understanding

Richard Skemp (1976) drew a sharp line between two kinds of mathematical understanding:

**Instrumental** — knowing a rule that produces an answer ("move the 3 to the other side") without knowing why. Feels efficient; is brittle. Discarded after the moment, builds no foundation for unfamiliar problems. Most school instruction produces this, because it's faster to test and teach.

**Relational** — "knowing what to do *and* why" — a connected mental model the learner can generate their own solutions from, for problems they've never seen. Takes longer to build; transfers broadly.

Self-learners with no instructor are especially at risk: they may not know which kind of understanding they have until a novel problem exposes the gap.

**Implication:** Never teach a step as a bare instruction. Teach the reasoning that *produces* the step, and name the thinking move so it transfers.

## The illusion of competence

Learners systematically overestimate what they understand. A clear explanation *feels* like learning, so "I followed that" gets mistaken for "I can do that." Recognition (following along) and recall (reproducing from memory) feel identical in the moment; they are not. Only recall and transfer build durable ability.

Research on AI-assisted learning makes this worse: fluent, frictionless output is easy to accept passively. Students who lean on AI for answers show measurable declines in their own ability.

The fix is not to be a worse explainer. It is to **make the learner retrieve and reconstruct**, not just receive.

**Implication:** End concept explanations with reconstruction tasks and twist problems, not "does that make sense?" The goal is a learner who needs you slightly less each session.

## Productive struggle vs. unproductive frustration

Struggle is good — it signals the brain that something is worth encoding. But there is a threshold past which struggle becomes demoralization: the learner stops trying, not because the problem is hard, but because they feel incapable.

Calibrating this is the central skill of a tutor:
- Hints not landing after two turns → the break is lower than you thought; drop a rung on the ladder and rediagnose.
- Rising emotional language ("I'm stupid," "I'll never get this") → redirect to the concrete immediately; one achievable sub-task.
- Repeated identical error after two corrections → it's a misconception, not a slip; consult `references/misconceptions.md` and engineer a contradiction.

## Why "does that make sense?" fails

It is a yes/no question with strong social pressure toward yes. The learner who is lost says yes because saying no feels like admitting failure. The learner who understood also says yes. The tutor receives zero information.

Replace it with reconstruction: "Without scrolling up, what were the two key moves?" This question cannot be answered with a social reflex — it requires retrieval. A blank or uncertain answer is the signal you needed.
